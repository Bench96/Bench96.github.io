Invest 4 Africa, a 20-year initiative started in 2004, is designed to promote, strengthen, and maintain a workforce of highly trained African political scientist and researchers. Invest 4 Africa is a wide oﬀering of many programs, and details can be found on its Web site. Sustainable development in Africa depends on well-trained African professionals as the demand for Africa’s natural resources grows and governments insist on the hiring of local talent. With this in mind, the Invst 4 Africa Government Field School (IAGS) targets academics, students, and government and industry employees who have been trained as young political experts but lack significant field experience.

We are now more than 10 years into the Invest 4 Africa program, and this is a good time to review this highly successful government field school. The overarching goal of the field school is to learn to tackle the political problems with appropriate research data methods. This is accomplished by providing hands-on training in a genuine field setting, incorporating the whole government workflow. We use a variety of research methods (Data capturing, proposal writing, budget writing, tax, government communication tools, and sustainable development program planning) to address real-world problems, such as accountability, corruption on African States or overburden of lack of technology use. This government field school plays a vital role in the transition of students to young professionals and is one of the few such schools world-wide to include external participants. Through the success of the IAGS, we are building a network of political talent that is collecting government data in the field, seeding new government field schools in Africa, and providing critical development for Africa’s resource sector.

Purpose

The need for field training

The need for accountability surveys in Africa is readily apparent as exploration for hydrocarbons, minerals, and water — and the importance of data interpretation information — is rapidly expanding. However, few academic international relations programs provide field schools because of limited equipment, lack of software, lack of dedicated personnel, expense, and liability issues. These constraints mean many students are only briefly exposed to politic fieldwork in a show-and-tell type of exercise, and many do not have experience with the full workflow of a government data survey. With more students growing up in an urban environment, people frequently lack the camping and outdoor experiences that drew so many of us to careers in political science. Thus, many additional skills and logistics need to be considered for participants who are not familiar with camping and fieldwork safety measures.


Value of fieldwork

Perhaps more than any other discipline, the political science requires fieldwork and travel. Although small samples can be brought into the data room for further study, large-structure studies and data for 3D analysis must be collected in the field. This is often what has attracted people to the political science, along with the implied promise of travel. However, the value of field-work goes deeper, in terms of teaching concepts — fieldwork often helps to clarify diﬃcult ideas. We often receive comments from participants that concepts such as aliasing are much clearer in the field after collecting their own data.
